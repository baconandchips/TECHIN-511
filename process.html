Remote Control Holder: by Team 11

Remote Control Holder

Design Processes

Team members: Guanting Li and Xuefei Chen

Description of Design

Through 5 weeks, we iterated through many different products. We learned that a simple remote control holder actually involves a lot of thought process between MVP and actual final iteration (and beyond, since there is always room for further improvement).

Week 1

Drawing inspiration first from sponge holders, we briefly entertained the idea of making a better sponge holder, before proceeding onto the idea of creating a remote control holder. (Rightmost sponge-holder dispenses soap onto sponge.)

On the next step, we attempted to draw inspiration from products currently on the market, such as an ergonomic remote control that doesn't get stuck in between sofas (left), a remote control holder that sticks to walls (center), and a bucket that can potentially hold remote controllers (right).

After a brief round of considerations for making remote controls more easily found by attaching a stand on the back, we decided to pivot and find a better method of mitigating remote control loss for ourselves. Having a floating basket powered by a motor and stabilized by a magnet turned out to also be a not-so-great idea.

Lots of brainstorming was done to generate ideas. Visiting Justin's office hours and receiving input from their instruction has been helpful as well. From there, we made some final ideas ready for our presentation with Justin on Friday.

We presented a preliminary model for remote control holders (center) for our presentation; Justin's suggestions led us to create a more hang-able holder.

Our biggest challenge this week was landing on one single project and one single idea for our project. Learning this process has been fun however.

Week 2-3

For these weeks, we took a focus around challenges, and solved these challenges amongst ourselves and with the teaching team.

During this week, we designed a first version of our prototype for testing. It was bright orange and very effective on the MKRSPC tables! It was too small in diameter/accommodation for own my table thickness, however. Our biggest lesson here is that asking for help early and often can help yield high rewards.

Through these weeks, the most important challenge that we faced included being able to find stability in our design, while ensuring that the design has a snug fit with the table, no matter its length. Our lesson here is that solving designs through challenges can be very effective. After week 3, one of the instructors suggested having an accelerated pace of iterations done; this train of thought spurred us to create much more than we could have imagined before.
